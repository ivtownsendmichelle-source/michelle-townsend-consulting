## Three Real Paths

You have three ways to get started. They are not ranked. They cost different things. Pick the one that fits where you are right now.

### Path 1: Find a Fiscal Sponsor

A fiscal sponsor is a nonprofit that already exists. They let you run your project under their name. Grant money goes to them. They pass it to your project minus a small fee, usually 7 to 15 percent.

You do not form an organization. You do not file with the IRS. You just need a local nonprofit that believes in what you are trying to do.

**What it costs you:** A few weeks of conversations. You need to find the right org and pitch your idea clearly enough that they say yes. The Nonprofit Association of Oregon has a guide on how this works. Ore-Cal RC&D in Southern Oregon offers fiscal sponsorship for rural community projects.

**Where to start Monday:** Ask Michelle for an intro to local orgs that might sponsor you. Write one paragraph about what you want to build and who it helps. That is your pitch.

### Path 2: Start Your Own Nonprofit

Form a 501(c)(3). It is not as hard as it sounds. Oregon's process is manageable. Once you have it, every grant on the list is open to you. You control everything.

**What it costs you:** Time. Expect a few months from start to finish. You need to file articles of incorporation with Oregon, apply to the IRS for tax-exempt status, set up a board of directors (minimum three people in Oregon), and open a bank account. There are filing fees. The IRS Form 1023-EZ costs $275 if your budget will be under $50,000 a year, which yours almost certainly will be at the start.

**Where to start Monday:** Talk to Michelle about whether this is the right move for your situation. If your idea is clear and you have two or three people willing to sit on a board, it might be. If your idea is still forming, Path 1 or Path 3 might be smarter first.

### Path 3: Do a Service Year

Join AmeriCorps VISTA. One year. Full time. You get placed at a nonprofit that fights poverty. You learn how grants work, how to recruit volunteers, how to run projects. No degree required. You are 18, so you qualify today.

You get a living allowance, health benefits, and a choice at the end: $7,395 toward education or $1,800 cash.

**What it costs you:** A year. You will not make much money. The living allowance covers basics, not extras. But you come out the other side with skills, connections, and a resume that makes the rest of this list a lot easier.

**Where to start Monday:** Go to americorps.gov and search for VISTA positions in Southern Oregon or wherever you are willing to go. Apply to two or three. The process takes a few weeks.

## The Strategic Picture

Here is the honest situation.

You are 18 with no organization behind you in a town of 1,700 people. That is not a weakness. The Illinois Valley is small enough that one person with a clear idea can actually move things. But you need to be realistic about the order of operations.

**The smartest first move is fiscal sponsorship.** It gets you funded fastest with the least overhead. You do not need a board. You do not need a tax ID. You need one existing nonprofit that says yes.

The Illinois Valley Community Response Team already does community-level work in Cave Junction. They are worth a conversation. Other Josephine County organizations may also be open to it.

**If your idea is big enough to need its own org, do that second.** Get a project running under a sponsor first. Prove the idea works. Then incorporate. Funders and board members are a lot easier to recruit when you can show them something real.

**AmeriCorps is the best move if you are not sure what to build yet.** It is not a detour. It is training. You will come out of it knowing more about how nonprofits actually work than most people learn in four years of school.

**The grants worth watching once you have an org:**

- Oregon Community Foundation Community Grants. Spring 2026 cycle prioritizes small rural orgs. This is your backyard.
- Ford Family Foundation. Based in Roseburg. Funds youth development and community capacity in rural Oregon towns under 35,000. Illinois Valley qualifies.
- Reser Family Foundation Small Community Initiative. Up to $15,000 a year for rural community development.
- Oregon Youth Development Division grants for ages 6 to 24.

**The long shots:**

- Echoing Green Fellowship. $100,000 for early-stage social entrepreneurs. Competitive. You need a clear plan.
- Thiel Fellowship. $200,000 for people under 22 who want to build instead of go to college. Very competitive. Worth knowing about.

## What Not to Do

Most young founders make the same mistakes. Here is what to skip.

**Do not start by forming a nonprofit.** Filing paperwork feels productive. It is not. A 501(c)(3) is a container. If you do not know exactly what goes in it yet, the container is just overhead. Board meetings, IRS filings, state reports. All for a project that does not exist yet. Start with a fiscal sponsor or a service year. Incorporate when you have something to incorporate around.

**Do not write a grant before you have a project.** Grants fund work that is already happening or ready to launch. They do not fund ideas. Funders want to see that you have done something. Even something small. A pilot, a survey, a community event. Proof that this is real and you are the person to do it.

**Do not try to solve everything.** Pick one problem. Serve one group of people. Do it well enough that someone notices. Then grow. The organizations that try to do five things at launch usually do none of them.

**Do not skip the board question.** If you do form a nonprofit, you need a board. Not your three best friends. People who bring something you do not have. Someone who knows money. Someone who knows the community. Someone who has run something before. A bad board is worse than no board because it gives you the illusion of structure with none of the substance.

**Do not assume you need money to start.** The best first step for most community projects is free. Knock on doors. Talk to people. Find out what they actually need. Show up at a town meeting. Volunteer somewhere. The work starts before the funding. If you cannot get people interested without money, money will not fix that.

**Do not go quiet.** Tell people what you are doing. Not to impress them. Because you need them. You need a mentor. You need a fiscal sponsor. You need board members, volunteers, and eventually donors. None of that happens if nobody knows you exist.
