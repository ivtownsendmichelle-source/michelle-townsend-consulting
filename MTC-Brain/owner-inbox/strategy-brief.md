# Strategy Brief: Room to Breathe
## House Cleaning + Organization for Elderly and Disabled Clients
### Cave Junction and Grants Pass, Oregon

**Prepared:** April 10, 2026

---

## Positioning Statement

Room to Breathe is a house cleaning and home organization service built for elderly and disabled adults in Cave Junction and Grants Pass. We do what general cleaning companies skip: we organize, we adapt to mobility needs, and we show up in rural communities where no one else does.

---

## Brand Voice Guide

1. **Sound like a neighbor, not a company.** Write the way you would talk to someone at the farmers market. First person. Short sentences. No polish.

2. **Never use clinical language.** No "clients," "aging population," or "service delivery." Say "the people we help." Say "your mom." Say "their home."

3. **Be specific, not sentimental.** Instead of "we care about seniors," say "we reorganized June's kitchen so she could reach her coffee mugs without a step stool." Details build trust. Generalities sound like ads.

4. **Show the work.** Talk about what you actually do in a home. Moving the heavy stuff. Clearing out the junk drawer. Wiping down the grab bars. People want to picture you in their parent's house.

5. **Respect dignity.** Never frame the people you serve as helpless. They are adults whose homes got ahead of them. That happens to everyone. You are an extra pair of hands, not a rescue.

6. **Local is the point.** Mention Cave Junction. Mention Grants Pass. Mention the drive. Mention the weather. The more specific to this place, the more real you sound.

7. **No fake urgency.** No "don't wait," no "limited spots," no "act now." This audience has heard enough sales pressure for a lifetime. Just be useful.

## Platform Strategy

### Facebook: The Engine

Facebook is where this business lives. The target audience (65+, their adult children, local community members) uses Facebook more than any other platform. Cave Junction and Grants Pass both have active community groups where word-of-mouth spreads fast.

**Job:** Build local trust. Get shared in community groups. Be the name people mention when someone asks "does anyone know a good cleaner for my mom?"

**Tactics:**
- Post in your own business page, then share to community groups where allowed
- Stories about real work (anonymized). Before/after framing without being gimmicky.
- Practical tips that show expertise without selling
- Engage with comments. Answer questions in groups even when they are not about you.

### Instagram: The Portfolio

Instagram is secondary but important for adult children (ages 35-55) who are managing care for aging parents. They search, scroll, and vet services visually.

**Job:** Show the work. Build credibility through visuals. Let people see what a clean, organized home looks like when someone who understands elderly needs does the job.

**Tactics:**
- Before/after photos (with permission)
- Close-up shots of organized spaces
- Short captions that tell a story
- Hashtags that combine local + niche

### LinkedIn: The Referral Network

LinkedIn is not for finding customers directly. It is for connecting with the people who refer customers: case managers, social workers, AAA staff, healthcare providers, senior living facility directors.

**Job:** Establish professional credibility. Position the business as a referral partner for the aging services network in Josephine County.

**Tactics:**
- Share insights about gaps in rural elderly care
- Talk about what you see in the field (without identifying anyone)
- Connect with RVCOG staff, Josephine County social services, local healthcare providers
- Occasional posts about the business side of doing this work in a rural area

### Twitter/X: The Amplifier

Twitter/X has the smallest direct audience for this business, but it is useful for broader discovery, connecting with advocacy communities, and putting the business name in front of journalists, policy people, and disability advocates.

**Job:** Short, specific, local-pride posts. Get found by people outside the immediate community who care about rural aging, disability services, and gaps in care.

**Tactics:**
- Short observations about rural life and aging
- Local pride. Mention the community by name.
- Link to longer content on other platforms when relevant

## 30-Day Content Arc

### Week 1 (Days 1-7): "Who We Are"

Introduce the business. Say what you do and who you do it for. No selling. Just clarity. Let people understand what this is.

- Day 1: The founding story. Why this business exists.
- Day 2: What we actually do in a home (the specifics)
- Day 3: Why elderly and disabled clients need something different
- Day 4: Cave Junction and why we are here
- Day 5: A tip about home organization for limited mobility
- Day 6: What "cleaning" means when you are working around a walker
- Day 7: A quiet post. Something about home. What it means to feel comfortable in your own space.

### Week 2 (Days 8-14): "The Work"

Show what you do. Real talk about real situations (anonymized). Build credibility through specifics.

- Day 8: A before/after story (not photos yet, just the narrative)
- Day 9: The most common thing families ask for
- Day 10: A practical tip about kitchen organization for seniors
- Day 11: What we do differently from a regular cleaning service
- Day 12: The hardest part of this work (honest, not dramatic)
- Day 13: Why organization matters as much as cleaning
- Day 14: A post about trust. Why consistency matters (same cleaner every time).

### Week 3 (Days 15-21): "The Community"

Connect the business to the community it serves. Show you belong here.

- Day 15: What rural communities do for their elders (and where the gaps are)
- Day 16: A resource post. Oregon programs that help fund in-home services.
- Day 17: What adult children should know when their parents need help
- Day 18: A Cave Junction/Grants Pass appreciation post
- Day 19: How to talk to your parent about getting help at home
- Day 20: Partner spotlight or shout-out to a local org that serves seniors
- Day 21: A rest-day post. Something human. Something small.

### Week 4 (Days 22-30): "The Invitation"

Soft ask. Make it easy for people to reach out. Answer objections before they ask.

- Day 22: What a first visit looks like
- Day 23: Answering "how much does it cost?"
- Day 24: A post addressing common concerns (trust, access, strangers in the home)
- Day 25: A testimonial or story from someone you have helped
- Day 26: Why families call us (the adult child perspective)
- Day 27: A seasonal or timely post (spring cleaning, weather prep, etc.)
- Day 28: What we wish more people knew about aging in place
- Day 29: A simple post: here is how to reach us
- Day 30: Looking ahead. What we are building. Gratitude for the community.

## Hook Lines

These opening lines work across platforms. Adjust length for the format.

1. **"My mom's house used to smell like lavender and fresh bread. Last time I visited, it smelled like something was wrong."** (Adult child perspective. Visceral. Real.)

2. **"Nobody tells you that the hardest part of getting older is not being able to keep your own house the way you want it."** (Dignity-first framing. Speaks to the person being served.)

3. **"We drove 30 miles down the Illinois Valley last Tuesday because nobody else would."** (Rural coverage. Specific. Shows commitment.)

4. **"There are 24,000 people over 65 in Josephine County. Most of the cleaning companies around here were not built for any of them."** (Data point that earns attention. Positions the gap.)

5. **"She told me she had not had anyone in her kitchen in two years. Not because she did not want help. Because she did not know who to trust."** (Trust as the core barrier. Story-driven.)
