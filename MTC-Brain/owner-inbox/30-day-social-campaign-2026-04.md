DAY 8 — Wednesday — PRIMARY POST

FACEBOOK:
Myth: "We are too small to need an AI policy."

If anyone on your team is using ChatGPT, Grammarly, Canva's AI features, or any AI writing tool, you already have AI in your organization. You just do not have a policy for it.

Here is what that looks like in practice. A staff member pastes donor names and giving amounts into ChatGPT to draft a thank-you letter. That data is now in a system you do not control. A volunteer uses AI to write part of a grant application and nobody reviews it for accuracy. A board member asks an AI tool to summarize confidential meeting notes.

None of these people did anything malicious. They were trying to save time. But without a policy, there is no guardrail. And when something goes wrong, the org is the one holding the bag.

You do not need a 40-page document. You need a clear, short agreement about what tools are approved, what data never goes into them, and who is responsible for checking AI-generated work.

DAY 14 — Tuesday — PRIMARY POST

FACEBOOK:
Five things your organization should decide about AI before the end of this month.

1. Which AI tools are approved for use. Just name them. ChatGPT, Grammarly, whatever your team uses. Write them down.

2. What data never goes into an AI tool. Donor names, client information, financial records, anything covered by your confidentiality agreements.

3. Who reviews AI-generated content before it goes out. Grant language, emails to funders, public communications. Someone has to read it.

4. Where AI use gets documented. Even if it is a shared spreadsheet. "I used ChatGPT for X on this date" is better than nothing.

5. When this policy gets revisited. Set a date. Quarterly is fine. Put it on the calendar.

None of these require special training. None of them cost money. They require a conversation and about two hours.

DAY 21 — Tuesday — PRIMARY POST

FACEBOOK:
Here is a pattern I have seen more than once.

A nonprofit gets a grant that requires reporting on outcomes. Staff are overwhelmed. Someone suggests using AI to help write the reports. The AI produces something that looks polished and professional. It goes out.

Later, someone notices the AI-generated report included a statistic that does not match the org's actual data. Or it framed an outcome in a way that overstates the impact. Not because anyone lied. Because the AI filled in gaps the way AI does. Confidently and incorrectly.

This is not a horror story. Nobody got defunded. But it was a close call. And it happened because there was no step in the process where a human checked the AI's work against the actual numbers.

The fix is simple. Every piece of AI-generated content that goes outside your organization gets reviewed by a person who knows the real data. Write that into your workflow. Make it non-negotiable.
